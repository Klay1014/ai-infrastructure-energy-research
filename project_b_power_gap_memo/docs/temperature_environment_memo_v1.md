# Temperature Risk And Electricity Demand In Taiwan

Subtitle: An Environmental Signal Memo Linking Heat, Cooling Load, Peak Demand, And Power Adequacy

## 1. Executive Summary

Taiwan's electricity adequacy should be analyzed not only through annual demand growth, but also through temperature-driven peak load. Rising temperature matters because heat affects cooling demand, high-temperature health risk, labor conditions, data center cooling, and the timing of electricity demand. Public climate sources show that Taiwan is experiencing warming and more high-temperature risk, while power-system sources show that Taiwan's dispatch challenge increasingly depends on peak and night-peak conditions.

This memo is temperature-first. It does not claim that Taiwan will definitely face an electricity shortage. Instead, it asks how temperature signals convert into power-system stress. The core chain is:

```text
rising temperature
-> more hot days, heat waves, and warm nights
-> higher cooling demand
-> higher afternoon and evening load
-> pressure on reserve margin, grid operation, and vulnerable groups
```

The strongest public evidence supports three cautious conclusions:

1. Taiwan's recent temperature baseline is warmer than historical climate normals.
2. Extreme heat indicators, not only annual average temperature, are the right environmental signals to track.
3. Electricity risk is most likely to appear through peak load and night-peak pressure, not simply annual electricity totals.

## 2. Temperature Baseline

CWA's 2024 Taiwan climate analysis reports that Taiwan's 2024 annual mean temperature was 24.6°C, 0.7°C above the climate normal, ranking first in Taiwan's meteorological record. This is a useful baseline, but it is not enough for power-system analysis. Electricity demand responds more strongly to hourly and daily heat exposure than to annual averages.

For that reason, this memo should track indicators such as:

- Days above 36°C
- High-temperature warning frequency
- Warm nights
- Heat wave duration
- Cooling degree days
- Urban heat-island exposure

CWA's high-temperature warning system is a practical public signal. It uses yellow, orange, and red warning thresholds based on temperatures such as 36°C and 38°C and their duration. These thresholds matter because they are closer to human heat stress and cooling behavior than annual mean temperature.

## 3. Temperature-To-Electricity Mechanism

Temperature affects electricity through several channels:

- Residential air conditioning
- Commercial HVAC
- Industrial cooling and process control
- Data center cooling
- Evening cooling demand after solar output falls
- Heat stress affecting labor, public health, and cooling access

The key analytical point is that temperature affects peak load differently from annual electricity demand. A hotter year can raise total consumption, but a hot afternoon or warm night can stress the system even if annual supply is adequate.

A simplified framework:

```text
temperature-sensitive load =
cooling adoption
x cooling usage intensity
x building efficiency
x humidity and heat stress
x industrial/data-center cooling needs
```

This should be treated as a framework unless supported by historical load and temperature data.

## 4. Taiwan Power-System Link

MOEA and Taipower state that Taiwan's 2026 peak load is expected to reach 42.94 GW, with night peak load at 38.53 GW. They also state that new renewable energy and four new units totaling 5.2 GW are planned, with day peak reserve above 10% and night peak reserve above 7%.

These are important official baseline signals, but they should not end the analysis. A temperature-first memo should stress-test the system under heat scenarios:

- What happens if high-temperature days cluster during low wind or reduced hydro conditions?
- What happens if warm nights keep cooling load high after solar output falls?
- What happens if industrial cooling and data center cooling rise faster than expected?
- What happens if urban heat islands concentrate demand in distribution-constrained areas?

Taipower's own public materials describe a shift from day-peak challenges toward night-peak challenges as solar output supports daytime demand but creates more evening dispatch pressure. This makes warm nights especially important.

## 5. Scenario Framework

Avoid a fake-precise forecast. Use scenarios.

### Scenario A: Normal Hot Summer

Temperature is above normal but within recent experience. Cooling demand rises, but reserve margin remains within official planning expectations.

Signal:

- High-temperature warnings occur but do not cluster for long periods.
- Night peak remains near official baseline.
- Reserve margin remains above threshold.

### Scenario B: Heat Wave Plus Warm Nights

High daytime temperature is followed by elevated nighttime minimum temperature. Cooling demand persists into evening hours after solar output declines.

Signal:

- More warm nights
- Higher night peak
- Lower night reserve margin
- More demand response activation

### Scenario C: Heat Wave Plus Supply Stress

Heat occurs during lower renewable output, plant outage, transmission constraint, or fuel-supply stress.

Signal:

- High-temperature warning plus low reserve margin
- Higher electricity price or emergency dispatch signal
- Public appeals for demand reduction

### Scenario D: Structural Heat-Driven Load Growth

More frequent high-temperature days and higher cooling adoption raise baseline summer electricity demand.

Signal:

- Cooling degree days trend upward
- Summer peak load rises faster than annual demand
- Night peak becomes more frequent and binding

## 6. Risk Matrix

| Risk | Environmental Signal | Electricity Signal | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Afternoon cooling peak | Days above 36°C | Higher day peak load | Cooling demand stresses peak capacity |
| Warm-night pressure | High minimum temperature | Higher night peak | Demand persists after solar declines |
| Heat wave clustering | Multi-day high-temperature alerts | Lower reserve margin across several days | Less recovery time for system operation |
| Urban heat island | City-level heat exposure | Distribution-level load concentration | Local grid stress may matter more than national supply |
| Cooling inequality | Heat exposure plus low cooling access | Health risk, not just electricity risk | Environmental risk is also social risk |

## 7. What To Track Next

Temperature signals:

- Monthly temperature anomaly
- Days above 36°C
- Warm nights
- Heat wave duration
- High-temperature alerts
- Cooling degree days

Power signals:

- Taipower daily peak load
- Day peak versus night peak
- Operating reserve margin
- Solar generation and evening ramp
- Demand response activation
- New capacity and storage additions

Social/environmental signals:

- Heat injury reports
- Urban heat island maps
- Cooling access and energy burden
- Outdoor labor exposure

## 8. Limitations

Temperature is not the only driver of electricity demand. Humidity, income, air-conditioner adoption, building efficiency, industrial activity, electricity prices, holidays, and demand response all matter. Public data may also fail to isolate data center cooling load or appliance-level electricity use.

The memo should therefore classify temperature-power risk direction rather than claim exact MW per degree unless a specific data model is built from historical CWA temperature and Taipower load data.
