# Temperature-First Environmental Report Outline

Title: Temperature Risk And Electricity Demand In Taiwan

Subtitle: An Environmental Signal Memo Linking Heat, Cooling Load, Peak Demand, And Power Adequacy

## Project Direction

This is the environmental branch of the Taiwan power adequacy project. The main subject is temperature, not electricity. Electricity appears as the key socioeconomic transmission channel:

```text
Rising temperature
-> more hot days, heat waves, warm nights, and heat stress
-> higher cooling demand
-> higher afternoon and evening electricity load
-> pressure on peak reserve, night peak, grid, and vulnerable groups
```

The goal is not to prove that Taiwan will run out of electricity. The goal is to build a public-signal framework for how temperature risk converts into power-system stress.

## Research Question

How does rising temperature in Taiwan affect cooling demand, peak electricity load, and power adequacy risk, and what public signals should be tracked to distinguish normal summer variability from structural heat-driven electricity stress?

## Core Sections

### 1. Executive Summary

Answer in one page:

- Taiwan is warming, and recent official climate materials show more high-temperature risk.
- Temperature matters for electricity because cooling demand is strongly weather-sensitive.
- The most important power-system risk is not only annual electricity consumption, but peak load and night peak during hot periods.
- The memo should track heat signals and power signals together.

### 2. Temperature Baseline

Questions:

- What does recent Taiwan temperature data show?
- Are hot days, heat events, warm nights, or heat-stress signals increasing?
- Which indicators matter most for electricity demand?

Key indicators:

- Annual mean temperature
- Monthly mean temperature anomalies
- Days above 36°C
- Heat wave duration
- Warm nights / high minimum temperature
- Heat stress index or high-temperature warning frequency

### 3. Why Temperature Affects Electricity

Questions:

- How does temperature convert into electricity demand?
- Why is cooling load more important than annual average temperature?
- Why do warm nights matter?

Mechanisms:

- Residential and commercial air conditioning
- Industrial cooling and process temperature control
- Data center cooling
- Heat-driven evening load persistence
- Lower thermal efficiency or derating risk for some power equipment

### 4. Taiwan Power-System Link

Questions:

- Does heat stress affect annual electricity, peak load, or night peak most?
- How do high-temperature days interact with solar generation?
- What is the role of demand response and storage?

Signals:

- Taipower daily peak load and reserve margin
- Day peak versus night peak
- High-temperature warnings
- Solar output and evening ramp
- Demand response activation

### 5. Environmental And Social Risk

Questions:

- Who is most exposed to high temperatures?
- Does cooling access create inequality?
- Are there health, labor, and urban heat-island implications?

Signals:

- Heat alerts
- Heat injury data if public
- Urban heat island studies
- Outdoor worker exposure
- Elderly and low-income cooling vulnerability

### 6. Temperature-To-Load Scenario Framework

Do not use a fake-precise forecast. Use scenarios:

```text
Temperature stress scenario =
hot-day frequency
x cooling adoption / usage
x building efficiency
x industrial/data-center cooling intensity
x demand response capability
```

Power adequacy link:

```text
heat-driven peak pressure =
temperature-sensitive load
- demand response
- storage / flexible cooling
- available reserve margin
```

### 7. Risk Matrix

| Temperature Signal | Power-System Interpretation | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| More days above 36°C | More cooling load | Higher peak pressure |
| Warm nights | Cooling demand persists after solar falls | Higher night-peak risk |
| Humid heat | Higher heat-stress and cooling need | Health and electricity pressure |
| Heat waves during low wind / low hydro | Supply and demand stress align | Reserve margin risk |
| Urban heat island | Localized cooling load concentration | Distribution grid stress |

### 8. What To Track Next

- CWA high-temperature alerts
- Monthly temperature anomalies
- Warm-night indicators
- Cooling degree days
- Taipower daily peak load
- Day/night reserve margin
- Solar generation and evening ramp
- Demand response and storage events
- Heat injury or labor exposure data

### 9. Limitations

- Temperature alone does not determine electricity demand.
- Humidity, income, AC adoption, building efficiency, industrial activity, and electricity prices matter.
- Public data may not reveal real-time appliance-level or data-center cooling load.
- The memo should classify risk direction, not estimate exact MW per degree unless supported by a credible source.
