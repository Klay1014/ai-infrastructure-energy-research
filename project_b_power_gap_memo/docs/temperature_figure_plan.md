# Temperature Report Figure Plan

## Figure 1: Temperature Risk Chain

Question:

How does temperature become a power-system issue?

Layout:

```text
Higher mean temperature
-> more hot days / warm nights / heat waves
-> cooling demand
-> afternoon peak and night peak
-> reserve margin pressure
-> health, labor, and grid risk
```

Takeaway:

Temperature is an environmental variable that becomes an infrastructure constraint through cooling demand.

## Figure 3: Temperature-To-Load Response

Question:

How could heat translate into electricity demand?

Chart type:

Conceptual response curve.

X-axis:

Daily maximum temperature or cooling degree days.

Y-axis:

Peak load or incremental cooling load.

Note:

Use as conceptual unless using verified historical temperature and Taipower load data.

Takeaway:

Electricity response to heat is likely nonlinear, especially when cooling demand rises during extreme heat.

## Figure 4: Day Peak Versus Night Peak

Question:

Why do warm nights matter?

Layout:

Show daily load shape:

- Afternoon heat peak
- Solar output during daytime
- Evening / night peak after solar falls
- Persistent cooling demand during warm nights

Takeaway:

Warm nights can create power-system pressure even after the hottest daytime hours pass.

## Figure 5: Temperature-Power Risk Matrix

| Temperature Condition | Power Condition | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| High heat, high solar | Daytime cooling load partly offset by solar | Afternoon load pressure, lower night relief |
| High heat, low solar | Cooling demand and low renewable output align | Reserve margin stress |
| Warm night, low wind | Cooling persists after solar falls | Night peak stress |
| Heat wave + outage | Demand high while supply is disrupted | Reliability risk |
| Heat wave + vulnerable groups | Cooling access varies by income and age | Health and equity risk |

Takeaway:

The highest-risk events occur when heat exposure and power-system constraints align.
