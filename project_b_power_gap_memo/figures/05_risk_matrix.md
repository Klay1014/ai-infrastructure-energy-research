# Figure 5: Temperature-Power Risk Matrix

| Risk | Temperature Signal | Electricity Signal | What It Means |
|---|---|---|---|
| Afternoon cooling peak | More 36 C+ days or high-temperature alerts | Higher daytime peak load | Cooling demand is stressing peak capacity |
| Warm-night pressure | Elevated nighttime minimum temperature | Higher night peak after solar falls | Evening flexibility becomes the constraint |
| Heat wave clustering | Multi-day heat warnings | Reserve margin falls across several days | The system gets less recovery time |
| Industrial load acceleration | Heat plus fab/data center growth | Official demand forecast revised upward | Demand baseline may be too low |
| Clean-power gap | High total load and slow renewable growth | More fossil dispatch or unmet corporate green-power demand | Total electricity and clean electricity diverge |
| Local grid stress | Urban heat or concentrated industrial load | Local delivery upgrades needed | National adequacy can hide local bottlenecks |

Takeaway: Track both environmental signals and power-system signals. A temperature event becomes infrastructure risk only when it aligns with load, reserve, grid, or clean-power constraints.
